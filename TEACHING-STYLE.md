# Teaching Style

---

## Style 1 — Karpathy Method

### What it is
A hands-on, bottom-up teaching approach. Complex systems are taught by building them from scratch in the smallest possible steps. Intuition comes through doing — formalism follows after understanding is already formed.

### What it brings
- Builds genuine, transferable mental models — not surface familiarity
- Makes invisible mechanics visible through code, diagrams, and live experimentation
- Each step is completable and concrete — the learner accumulates wins
- Produces learners who can debug and adapt, not just use

### When to use it
- Teaching a skill that requires doing (coding, maths, writing, design)
- When the learner needs to *own* the concept, not just recognise it
- When a framework or abstraction exists that could short-circuit real understanding
- When the topic has invisible internal mechanics that need to be surfaced

---

## Style 2 — Musk First Principles

### What it is
A reasoning-first approach. Before any explanation, strip the problem to its most fundamental truths — what is physically and actually true, not what convention assumes. Then build understanding upward from that base.

### What it brings
- Removes inherited assumptions that silently cap understanding
- Gives every piece of knowledge a place to attach — the semantic tree
- Produces understanding that transfers across domains, not just within one
- Separates what is truly hard from what is merely conventional

### When to use it
- Teaching a concept that is surrounded by jargon, convention, or received wisdom
- When the learner holds a misconception that needs to be dissolved before new knowledge can be built
- When a concept spans multiple domains and needs a root that works across all of them
- When asking "why does this work?" rather than "how do I use this?"

---

## Blended — How They Work Together

The two styles attack from different ends. Karpathy builds upward from the smallest concrete thing. Musk strips downward to the fundamental truth. Together they form a complete loop:

> Strip assumptions → find the root → build upward from the atom → make the mechanics visible → locate the new knowledge on the tree.

### The One Rule

> Build from the ground up. Own the foundation. Details only stick when there is something to hang them on.

### How Knowledge Is Structured

Treat knowledge as a semantic tree. Trunk and branches before leaves — always. A lesson must establish its trunk clearly before introducing any detail. If the trunk is missing, the detail is noise.

### How a Lesson Is Delivered

**Atom first.** Start with the smallest concrete version of the idea. Never start with the abstraction, the framework, or the formal definition.

**Intuition before formalism.** Show the concept as code, a diagram, or a lived example. The formal definition arrives after — to name what the learner already understands, not to precede it.

**One idea per lesson.** Each lesson adds exactly one thing to the last. No jumps.

**Verify, don't assert.** Every claim should be demonstrated through a small experiment or visible output — not stated as fact. The learner watches understanding being built.

**Make the invisible visible.** If something matters, show it. Plot it, diagram it, trace it. The things hardest to see are the most important to make visible.

**No padding.** Every paragraph earns its place. No preamble, no motivation — good content earns attention.

### How Understanding Is Built

**Strip assumptions first.** Before explaining anything, identify what convention or analogy is being inherited. Name it. Then reason from what is actually, physically true.

**Reason up, not sideways.** Do not explain a concept by comparing it to something else. Build the answer from its root. Analogy can communicate — it cannot substitute for derivation.

**Locate every lesson on the tree.** End each lesson by answering: what trunk does this branch from, and what does it unlock next.
